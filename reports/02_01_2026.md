## Conflict-Level Effects of Peace Agreements

### 1. Baseline Specification: Monthly Event Study
Figure 1 reports the event-study estimates. Pre-treatment coefficients fluctuate around zero and show no systematic trend, supporting the parallel trends assumption. Post-treatment estimates are negative, suggesting a reduction in violence following peace agreements, **but** the confidence intervals are wide and include zero in most periods.

### 2. Controlling for Macroeconomic Conditions

- We next augment the baseline specification by controlling for economic conditions using GDP per capita. 

- Annual GDP per capita series are linearly interpolated to the monthly level and included either as multiple lags or as a pre-treatment baseline value.

Figure 2 shows that including GDP controls does not materially affect either the magnitude or the precision of the estimated treatment effects.

This result is expected: in the Callaway–Sant’Anna framework, only pre-treatment values of covariates are used, and GDP per capita exhibits little variation within windows and limited predictive power for short-run changes in conflict intensity.

$$Cov (X_i​,\Delta Y_{it​}) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad Var(X_i | window) \approx 0 $$


### 3. Diagnosing the Source of Variability

To understand the lack of precision in monthly estimates, we examine within-window volatility in conflict fatalities. 

- Figure 3a reports the distribution of within-window volatility, measured as the standard deviation of log fatalities within each 36-month window. While many windows exhibit little variation, a substantial fraction displays extremely high volatility.

- Figure 3b plots the cross-sectional standard deviation of log fatalities by event time. Volatility remains high and relatively constant throughout the event window, indicating that peace agreements affect average violence but do not immediately stabilize conflict dynamics.

- Figure 3c compares six-month pre–post changes in conflict intensity between treated and control windows. Control windows remain tightly concentrated around zero, whereas treated windows exhibit substantial dispersion, with large reductions in violence for some agreements and increases for others.

### 4. Controlling for Pre-Treatment Conflict Dynamics

- A potential concern is that observed post-treatment declines in violence reflect mechanical mean reversion rather than causal effects of peace agreements. 

- To address this, we re-estimate the model controlling for pre-treatment conflict dynamics, measured as the average level of violence in the months preceding the agreement.

- Even after conditioning on pre-treatment conflict dynamics and mean reversion, the estimated treatment effects remain negative.
- While these controls do not substantially reduce standard errors—consistent with the high stochasticity of monthly conflict dynamics—they indicate that the estimated effects are not driven solely by mean reversion.


### 5. Temporal Aggregation of the Outcome

Given the high volatility of monthly fatalities, we re-estimate the model using quarterly aggregates of conflict fatalities. Temporal aggregation reduces idiosyncratic month-to-month shocks while preserving the timing of treatment.

Figure 4 presents the quarterly event-study estimates. Pre-treatment coefficients remain close to zero, while post-treatment effects are consistently negative and statistically significant. The average post-treatment effect implies a reduction in conflict fatalities of approximately 40 percent.

### 6. Smoothing Monthly Fatalities (MA3)

As an alternative to temporal aggregation, we estimate treatment effects using a backward-looking three-month moving average of monthly fatalities. This transformation smooths high-frequency noise without using post-treatment information.

Figure 5 shows that MA3 estimates closely mirror the quarterly results: pre-treatment effects are flat, and post-treatment coefficients indicate a persistent and statistically significant decline in violence. The estimated ATT implies a reduction of approximately 45 percent in average fatalities.
